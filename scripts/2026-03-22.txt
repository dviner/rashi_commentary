Parashah (Rashi): Tzav (Leviticus 6:1-8:36)
Date: 2026-03-22

Welcome to Rashi in Brief, the podcast where each week we explore the Torah portion through the eyes of the great medieval commentator, Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, known as Rashi. This week's portion is called Tzav, found in the book of Leviticus, chapters six through eight, and it's packed with details about sacrificial law that Rashi brings to life in surprising ways.

<break time="1.5s" />

Tzav picks up where last week's portion left off, diving deeper into the laws of the sacrificial offerings — the burnt offering, the meal offering, the sin offering, the guilt offering, and the peace offering. While the previous portion, Vayikra, introduced these offerings primarily from the perspective of the person bringing them, Tzav shifts focus to the priests, Aaron and his sons, telling them exactly how to perform these sacred rituals. The portion concludes with the dramatic seven-day consecration ceremony in which Moses installs Aaron and his sons into the priesthood before the entire assembled community. Now, you might think a portion filled with technical ritual instructions would leave little room for Rashi's interpretive genius, but it's actually the opposite. Rashi zeros in on words that seem redundant, phrases that appear unnecessary, and small grammatical choices that reveal hidden layers of meaning. He shows us that even in the most procedural passages, every word of the Torah is doing important work.

<break time="1.5s" />

Let's start right at the opening verse. God tells Moses, "Tzav et Aharon," meaning "Command Aaron," regarding the burnt offering. Rashi immediately asks: why does the Torah use the word "tzav," command, which is a strong, urgent term, instead of the softer "speak to" or "say to" that we see elsewhere? Rashi explains that whenever Scripture uses this word "command," it implies an urging — a push to act diligently, both immediately and for all future generations. Then he brings in Rabbi Shimon's teaching: this extra urging is especially necessary when the commandment involves a monetary loss. The burnt offering was entirely consumed on the altar — the priests received nothing from it. No meat, no portion. So there might naturally be less enthusiasm for performing it meticulously. Rashi is teaching us something profound about human nature right from the first word: even the most devoted servants of God need encouragement when the task costs them something personally.

<break time="0.75s" />

Next, let's look at what Rashi does with the priestly garments. The Torah says the priest must wear his "middo vad," his linen robe, when removing the ashes from the altar. Rashi notes that this garment is the same one called the "ketonet," the tunic, elsewhere. So why the different name here? Because the word "middo" comes from the root meaning "to measure" — the garment had to be tailored precisely to the priest's body. It couldn't be too long or too short. And when the Torah adds "al besaro," meaning "upon his flesh," Rashi reads this as requiring that nothing may come between the garment and the priest's skin. These seem like small details, but Rashi is drawing our attention to the idea that sacred service requires precision and intentionality, right down to how a garment fits.

<break time="0.75s" />

Now, here's one of my favorite Rashi comments in this portion. The Torah tells us that after removing the ashes in his linen garments, the priest must change into different clothes before carrying the ashes outside the camp. Rashi asks: why the costume change? Is it required by law? Not exactly, Rashi says. It's a matter of derech eretz — basic decency and propriety. He offers a beautiful analogy: the clothes in which a servant cooked a pot of food for his master should not be the same clothes in which he pours the master's wine. Ash removal is a humble, messy task. The priestly service at the altar is an exalted one. The same person does both, but the dignity of each task is honored by the change of garments. Rashi is reminding us that holiness lives in transitions, in how we move between the ordinary and the elevated.

<break time="0.75s" />

Let's turn to the meal offering. The Torah says, "V'zot torat ha-mincha," meaning "And this is the law of the meal offering." Rashi picks up on that word "torah," which literally means "instruction" or "law," and explains that whenever Scripture introduces a section with this word, it's meant to be inclusive — to establish one unified law for all types within that category. Why does this matter? Because in an earlier passage, the requirement for oil and frankincense in a meal offering was mentioned only in the context of a regular Israelite's offering, from which a priest takes a fistful. What about the meal offering of priests, which is entirely burned and from which no fistful is taken? Would it also require oil and frankincense? Thanks to this inclusive word "torah," Rashi says yes — one law governs all meal offerings. It's a masterful example of how Rashi uses a single word to solve a legal gap that a casual reader would never notice.

<break time="0.75s" />

One more comment worth exploring comes from the laws of the peace offering, the shelamim. The Torah describes the thanksgiving peace offering, the todah, brought by someone who has experienced a miraculous deliverance. Rashi specifies four categories of people who must bring this offering: those who crossed the sea safely, those who traversed a wilderness, those released from prison, and those who recovered from illness. He draws this from Psalm 107, which describes these four situations and calls on those who experienced them to give thanks to God. What's striking here is how Rashi transforms an abstract sacrificial law into something deeply personal and emotional. This isn't just a ritual procedure — it's about gratitude, about the human experience of surviving danger and standing before God to say thank you. Rashi also notes that unlike other peace offerings, which may be eaten over two days, the thanksgiving offering must be eaten in just one day and the following night. The practical effect of this tighter deadline, combined with the large quantity of bread that accompanies it, is that the person must share the meal widely, inviting others to join, thereby publicizing the miracle and spreading gratitude through community.

<break time="0.75s" />

Finally, let's look at the consecration ceremony at the end of the portion. Moses gathers the entire congregation to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting. Rashi notes something remarkable: this was a case where, as he puts it, "the lesser contained the greater" — a small space miraculously accommodated the entire nation. Then Moses says, "Zeh ha-davar," meaning "This is the thing which the Lord commanded me to do." Rashi reads an emotional undercurrent here. Moses is essentially telling the people: everything you are about to see me do — anointing my brother, dressing him in the sacred garments, installing him as High Priest — don't think I'm doing this for my own honor or for Aaron's glory. God commanded this. It's a moment of extraordinary humility, where Moses, the greatest prophet, makes clear that he is simply a faithful servant carrying out divine instructions.

<break time="1.5s" />

Stepping back from all of these individual comments, several themes emerge from Rashi's reading of Tzav. First, there is a deep concern with human motivation — the urging needed when personal cost is involved, the humility required in sacred service. Second, Rashi consistently shows that the Torah's precision is not accidental: every word choice, every seemingly redundant phrase, carries legal or moral significance. Third, and perhaps most beautifully, Rashi keeps pulling us from the abstract world of ritual law into the concrete world of human experience — the servant changing clothes, the person who survived illness gathering friends to share a feast of thanksgiving. His commentary transforms Leviticus from a manual of ancient procedures into a living document about how we approach God, serve others, and express gratitude.
