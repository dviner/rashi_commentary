Parashah (Rashi): Vayikra (Leviticus 1:1-5:26)
Date: 2026-03-15

Welcome to Rashi in Brief, where each week we explore the Torah portion through the eyes of the great medieval commentator, Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, known as Rashi. This week we're looking at Parshat Vayikra, the opening portion of the book of Leviticus, and as always, Rashi has so much to show us hiding beneath the surface of the text.

<break time="1.5s" />

Parshat Vayikra begins a dramatic shift in the Torah. We move from the sweeping narratives of Genesis and Exodus — the stories of creation, the patriarchs, the exodus from Egypt, the giving of the Torah — into what can feel like very different territory: the detailed laws of sacrifices and offerings. This portion lays out the procedures for burnt offerings of cattle, sheep, and birds, as well as various types of meal offerings and peace offerings. It's the kind of material that can feel technical and remote from our daily lives. But Rashi sees something else entirely. For him, almost every word in these passages is carefully chosen, and the seeming repetitions, the unusual grammar, the specific word choices — they're all doorways into deeper meaning. Rashi focuses here on questions like: why does the Torah use this particular word instead of another? Why does it repeat something that seems unnecessary? And what do these precise details reveal about the relationship between God and the people of Israel?

<break time="1.5s" />

Let's start at the very beginning, with the very first word of the book: Vayikra, meaning "And He called." The verse reads, "And the Lord called unto Moses and spoke unto him from the Tent of Meeting." Rashi immediately notices something. Why does the Torah say God "called" to Moses before speaking? Couldn't it just say God spoke to him? Rashi explains that every time God communicated with Moses — whether the Torah uses the word for speaking, saying, or commanding — it was always preceded by this calling. And this calling, Rashi tells us, is a way of expressing affection. It's the same language the angels use when they address one another, as we read in Isaiah: "And one called unto another and said, Holy, holy, holy." By contrast, when God appeared to the non-Jewish prophet Balaam, the Torah uses a very different word — vayikar — which implies something casual, even accidental. So right from the opening word, Rashi is telling us that this entire book, with all its detailed laws, is framed by a gesture of divine love and intimacy.

<break time="0.75s" />

Staying with that same first verse, Rashi picks up on a small but powerful word: eilav, meaning "to him." The verse says God called "to him" — to Moses specifically. Why stress this? Rashi says it's to exclude Aaron. Even though Aaron was the High Priest and deeply involved in the sacrificial service, these communications went to Moses alone. Furthermore, Rashi explains, the other Israelites couldn't even hear the sound of God's voice. The voice was miraculously powerful — the kind of voice described in the Psalms as breaking cedars — and yet it stopped precisely at the walls of the Tent of Meeting and went no further. It wasn't that the voice was quiet; it was that God contained it, directing it exclusively to Moses. This is a remarkable image: infinite power paired with infinite precision, a thunderous voice that somehow reaches only one person's ears.

<break time="0.75s" />

Now let's look at how the Torah introduces the laws of offerings themselves. The verse says, "Adam ki yakriv mikem," which literally translates as, "A man, when he offers from among you an offering." Rashi asks a characteristically sharp question: why does the Torah use the word adam for "man" here, when there are several other Hebrew words it could have used? His answer is beautiful. The word adam evokes Adam, the first human being. And what was unique about Adam? Everything in the world belonged to him. He could never have offered a stolen animal as a sacrifice, because nothing was stolen — it was all his. So too, Rashi says, you must never bring an offering from something acquired through robbery. With one word choice, the Torah reaches all the way back to the Garden of Eden to teach an ethical principle: the integrity of your offering depends on the integrity of how you obtained it.

<break time="0.75s" />

Moving to the meal offering, the mincha, Rashi makes one of his most beloved and moving observations. The verse says, "V'nefesh ki takriv," meaning, "And when a soul offers a meal offering." Rashi asks: why does the Torah use the word nefesh, meaning soul, here, when it doesn't use that word for any other type of voluntary offering? His answer is poignant. Who is it that typically brings a meal offering? The poor person. The meal offering is the most humble of all offerings — just flour, oil, and frankincense. No expensive animal. And God says, as it were: when a poor person brings this simple offering, I regard it as though that person has offered their very soul. The word nefesh is reserved for this offering precisely because it represents the greatest sacrifice — not in material terms, but in terms of what it costs the one who gives it.

<break time="0.75s" />

Finally, let's look at the bird offering, the offering of turtledoves or young pigeons. At the end of the passage describing how the bird is prepared, the Torah says to cleave it open "with its feathers." Rashi finds this puzzling. He points out that there is no person in the world who can smell the odor of burning feathers without being disgusted. So why would God command that the feathers be burned on the altar? His answer is touching: so that the altar should appear full and adorned with the sacrifice of the poor. The bird, with its feathers, makes a finer, more dignified appearance than without them. God cares about the dignity of the poor person's offering. And then Rashi drives the point home by noting that the Torah uses the identical phrase for the bird offering and the large animal offering — "a pleasing aroma to the Lord." Whether one offers much or little, it is equally pleasing to God, provided that one directs their heart to Heaven.

<break time="1.5s" />

Stepping back from these individual comments, we can see several powerful themes running through Rashi's reading of this portion. First, there is the theme of divine intimacy and care. From the very first word, Rashi frames the entire book of Leviticus not as a dry legal code but as a loving communication between God and Moses, and through Moses, to all of Israel. Second, there is a deep concern for ethics and integrity — the insistence that an offering must be honestly acquired, that the ritual act is meaningless without moral substance behind it. And third, perhaps most movingly, there is the theme of divine attention to the poor and the humble. Rashi consistently reads the text as showing that God values the sincerity of the giver over the size of the gift. In Rashi's hands, these laws of sacrifice become a profound meditation on what it means to approach the divine — with love, with honesty, and with a whole heart.
