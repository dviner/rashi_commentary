Parashah (Rashi): Shavuot II (Deuteronomy 14:22-16:17)
Date: 2026-05-17

Welcome to Rashi in Brief, where each week we explore the Torah portion through the eyes of the greatest Jewish Bible commentator, Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, known as Rashi. This week we're looking at a special reading for the holiday of Shavuot, drawn from Deuteronomy chapters fourteen through sixteen. Let's see what Rashi has to teach us.

<break time="1.5s" />

This reading covers a remarkable sweep of laws that together paint a picture of what a just and compassionate society looks like. We hear about tithing, the obligation to give a tenth of your produce. We hear about the sabbatical year, when debts are released. There are powerful passages about caring for the poor, about the treatment of Hebrew servants, about the firstborn animals dedicated to God, and about the three pilgrimage festivals, including Passover, Shavuot, and Sukkot. The thread running through all of it is the idea that how you handle your wealth, your time, and your relationships reflects your relationship with God. Rashi, as always, zeroes in on puzzling word choices, apparent contradictions between verses, and subtle grammatical clues that most readers would pass right over. In this portion especially, he draws out the practical and moral implications hidden in the Torah's precise language.

<break time="1.5s" />

Let's start with the very first verse of our reading. The Torah says, "You shall surely tithe all the increase of your seed." Rashi immediately asks a structural question: why is this law placed right here, right after the previous passage? What's the connection? His answer is striking. God is saying to Israel, in effect, do not force Me to send a scorching east wind to blast your grain while it's still ripening in the husk. If you tithe properly, your crops will be blessed. If you don't, they'll be destroyed before they even mature. Right from the start, Rashi frames tithing not merely as a legal obligation but as a relationship. Your generosity and God's blessing are bound together.

<break time="0.75s" />

Next, let's look at the laws about caring for the poor. The Torah says in one verse, "There shall be no needy among you," but just a few verses later it says, "The needy shall never cease out of the land." Rashi spots this glaring contradiction and resolves it beautifully. When you do the will of God, the needy will exist among others but not among you. But if you do not do God's will, then the needy will be among you. In other words, the first verse is a conditional promise, not a factual prediction. It depends entirely on how the community behaves. And Rashi adds a lovely grammatical note about the doubled verb form in the phrase "if you will carefully hearken." He says if a person listens even a little, if they show the initial determination to obey, they are gradually caused to listen to many things. One small act of attentiveness leads to a lifetime of deeper listening.

<break time="0.75s" />

Now let's turn to one of the most moving passages in the portion, the command to open your hand to the poor. The Torah says, "You shall not harden your heart nor close your hand from your needy brother." Rashi unpacks this with psychological precision. There are people, he says, who painfully deliberate whether to give or not, going back and forth in their minds. That's why the Torah says don't harden your heart. Then there are people who actually stretch out their hand, ready to give, but at the last moment pull it back and close it. That's why the Torah adds don't close your hand. Two different kinds of reluctance, two different warnings. And when the Torah says to provide "sufficient for his need, in that which he lacks," Rashi reads this expansively. Even a horse to ride on and a servant to run before him, if that's what the person was accustomed to. The obligation is not just to keep someone alive but to restore their dignity. Rashi even reads the Hebrew word "lo," meaning "for him," as a hint that you must help a person find a spouse, because the same word appears in Genesis when God says, "I will make a helper for him."

<break time="0.75s" />

Let's move to the laws of the Hebrew servant. When the Torah says the servant goes free after six years and commands, "You shall furnish him liberally," Rashi explains the unusual Hebrew word for this, ha'anek. He connects it to a word meaning something worn high on the body, something conspicuous. The idea is that your generosity to a departing servant should be visible, something that makes it obvious to everyone that you treated this person well. And when the Torah says, "Remember that you were a servant in Egypt," Rashi explains that God loaded the Israelites with good things not once but twice, first with the spoil of Egypt and then with the spoil at the Red Sea. So you too should load your servant with gifts, and then do it again.

<break time="0.75s" />

Now, a fascinating comment on the Passover offering. The Torah says, "You shall sacrifice the Passover offering unto the Lord your God, of sheep and oxen." But wait. Everyone knows the Passover lamb is a lamb, from the flock. So what are the oxen doing here? Rashi explains that the oxen refer to a separate festival offering, called the chagigah, that was brought alongside the Passover sacrifice. If a large group was sharing one lamb, an additional animal was slaughtered and eaten first so that the Passover lamb itself would be eaten after people were already satisfied, leisurely and with dignity, not wolfed down out of hunger. Even the way you eat a sacred meal matters.

<break time="0.75s" />

Finally, let's look at Rashi's comment near the end of the portion, about the festivals. The Torah lists four members of your household: your son, your daughter, your manservant, and your maidservant. Then it lists four vulnerable people in society: the Levite, the stranger, the orphan, and the widow. Rashi says these four are God's, corresponding to the four that are yours. If you gladden Mine, God says, I will gladden yours. It's a breathtaking formulation. The vulnerable in your community are not just your responsibility. They are, in a sense, God's own household. Joy is not a private affair. It's complete only when it's shared.

<break time="1.5s" />

Stepping back, what emerges from Rashi's commentary across this entire portion is a vision of a society built on reciprocity and attentiveness. Tithing, debt release, care for the poor, dignified treatment of servants, proper observance of festivals: these are not isolated laws. Rashi shows us that they are all connected by a single thread. How you treat the most vulnerable people around you is inseparable from your relationship with God. He also reveals something about his method. Rashi reads the Torah the way a jeweler examines a gem, turning it slowly, catching the light from every angle. A doubled verb, a seemingly redundant word, the placement of one law next to another, all of these carry meaning. Nothing in the text is accidental, and when you learn to read with that kind of care, the Torah opens up in ways you never expected.
