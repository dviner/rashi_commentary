Parashah (Rashi): Achrei Mot-Kedoshim (Leviticus 16:1-20:27)
Date: 2026-04-19

Welcome to Rashi in Brief, where each week we explore the Torah portion through the eyes of the great medieval commentator, Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, known as Rashi. This week we have a double portion called Achrei Mot-Kedoshim, which covers chapters sixteen through twenty of the book of Leviticus, and Rashi has so much to teach us about these rich and layered chapters.

<break time="1.5s" />

This double portion covers a remarkable range of material. Achrei Mot, meaning "after the death," opens in the aftermath of the tragic death of Aaron's two sons, Nadav and Avihu, who died after bringing an unauthorized offering before God. From that starting point, the Torah lays out the elaborate service of Yom Kippur, the Day of Atonement, when the High Priest enters the Holy of Holies, the innermost chamber of the Tabernacle. The portion then moves into laws about the proper place for offering sacrifices and the prohibition against consuming blood. Kedoshim, meaning "holy ones," shifts into a sweeping vision of holiness in everyday life, including the prohibition of following the immoral practices of Egypt and Canaan, and a detailed enumeration of forbidden sexual relationships. Rashi, characteristically, zeroes in on words that seem unnecessary, phrases that appear redundant, and logical gaps that invite closer reading. In this portion, he's especially attuned to why the Torah mentions certain details at all, and what we learn from each seemingly extra word.

<break time="1.5s" />

Let's begin right at the opening verse. The Torah says, "And the Lord spoke to Moses after the death of the two sons of Aaron." Rashi asks a deceptively simple question: why does the Torah need to mention that this happened after the death of Aaron's sons? The commandments about Yom Kippur could have been given without that reference. Rashi brings a beautiful parable from Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah: imagine two doctors visiting a sick patient. The first says, "Don't eat cold food or sleep in a damp place." The second says, "Don't eat cold food or sleep in a damp place, so that you don't die the way so-and-so died." The second doctor's warning is far more powerful because it invokes a real consequence. That's exactly what the Torah is doing here: by mentioning the death of Aaron's sons, God is telling Aaron, "Take this seriously — enter the Holy of Holies only as I command, so you don't die as your sons did." It's a reminder that the Torah isn't just giving instructions; it's speaking to real human beings who need motivation to listen carefully.

<break time="0.75s" />

Next, let's look at what the High Priest wears when he enters the Holy of Holies. The Torah specifies that he shall put on a linen tunic, linen breeches, a linen sash, and a linen turban. Rashi notes something fascinating: the High Priest normally wears eight garments, several of which contain gold. But when entering the Holy of Holies on Yom Kippur, he wears only four, all made of simple white linen, like an ordinary priest. Why? Rashi gives a stunning reason drawn from the Talmud: because the prosecuting counsel cannot serve as the defending counsel. Gold recalls the sin of the Golden Calf, and on the day when the High Priest is seeking forgiveness for the people, he cannot wear a reminder of their greatest sin. This small detail about clothing reveals a profound theological principle about how atonement works.

<break time="0.75s" />

Now let's turn to one of the most mysterious rituals in the Torah: the two goats of Yom Kippur. The Torah says Aaron shall cast lots upon two goats, one marked "for the Lord" and one marked "for Azazel." Rashi explains the procedure: one goat stands at the High Priest's right, one at his left. He reaches into an urn, draws a lot in each hand, and places them on the goats. But what is Azazel? Rashi explains that the word is a compound of two words meaning "strong" and "mighty," referring to a towering, craggy cliff, a precipitous and flinty rock. The goat for Azazel would be sent to this desolate place. Rashi then addresses another subtlety: the Torah says the goat for Azazel "shall be presented alive." Why mention alive? Isn't a goat standing there obviously alive? Rashi explains that this is to clarify its fate: since the Torah later says it will be "sent forth," we might think it's simply released into the wild to live freely. By emphasizing that it is presented alive now, the Torah implies that its sending forth is to death — it will be pushed off that towering cliff. Every word carries weight.

<break time="0.75s" />

Moving into the laws about blood, the Torah states, "For the life of the flesh is in the blood, and I have given it to you upon the altar to make expiation for your souls." Rashi draws out the logic beautifully: the life of every creature, not just sacrificial animals, depends on its blood. That is precisely why God designated blood for the altar, to atone for human life. As Rashi puts it, "Let life come and expiate for life." This isn't just a ritual rule; it's a window into the Torah's understanding of what sacrifice means. Blood represents life itself, and placing it on the altar is an act of substitution — life offered in place of life.

<break time="0.75s" />

In the transition to Kedoshim, the Torah warns, "After the deeds of the land of Egypt where you dwelt, you shall not do, and after the deeds of the land of Canaan where I bring you, you shall not do." Rashi makes a striking observation: the Torah isn't just saying Egypt and Canaan were bad. It's saying they were the most corrupt of all nations, and that the specific district of Egypt where the Israelites had lived was the most corrupt part of Egypt, and the specific Canaanite clans Israel would displace were the most corrupt among the Canaanites. In other words, the Israelites were coming from the worst and heading toward the worst. The Torah is acknowledging the enormous challenge of moral transformation — you've been surrounded by corruption, and you're about to be surrounded by more of it, so you must be especially vigilant.

<break time="0.75s" />

Finally, Rashi makes a crucial distinction between two categories of commandments. The Torah says, "You shall do My judgments and keep My ordinances." Rashi explains that "judgments," called mishpatim, are laws that align with basic human moral intuition, things like prohibitions against theft and murder that any society would recognize as just. "Ordinances," called chukim, are divine decrees that have no obvious rational explanation, like the prohibition against mixing wool and linen, or the laws of the Red Heifer. Rashi says that the evil inclination objects to these laws, asking, "Why should we observe them?" and the nations of the world also challenge them. That is precisely why the Torah adds, "I am the Lord" — I have decreed these, and you are not at liberty to exempt yourselves. This distinction is fundamental to how Rashi understands the entire Torah: some laws appeal to reason, others demand trust, and both require faithful observance.

<break time="1.5s" />

Stepping back, several themes emerge from Rashi's commentary across this double portion. One is the power of precision: Rashi shows again and again that the Torah wastes not a single word, and that every seemingly redundant phrase teaches something new. Another is the theme of moral seriousness: from the parable of the two doctors to the warnings about Egyptian and Canaanite practices, Rashi emphasizes that these laws are not abstract but urgent, addressed to people living in a morally challenging world. There is also a deep thread about the nature of holiness itself: it requires humility, as we see in the High Priest's simple linen garments; it requires obedience even when we don't understand, as we see in the distinction between mishpatim and chukim; and it requires vigilance, because the pull toward corruption is real and ever-present. Rashi's reading of this portion reminds us that Torah study is itself an act of paying attention, of refusing to let any detail pass by unexamined.
