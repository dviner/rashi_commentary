Parashah (Rashi): Ki Tisa (Exodus 30:11-34:35)
Date: 2026-03-08

Welcome to Rashi in Brief, where each week we explore the Torah portion through the brilliant and precise lens of Rashi, the great medieval commentator. This week's portion is Ki Tisa, found in the book of Exodus, chapters thirty through thirty-four, and it contains some of the most dramatic and heartbreaking moments in the entire Torah. Let's dive in and see what Rashi reveals.

<break time="1.5s" />

Ki Tisa covers an extraordinary range of material. It opens with God's instruction to Moses to take a census of the Israelites using half-shekel coins, then describes the copper laver for priestly washing, the sacred anointing oil, and the incense for the Tabernacle. We meet Bezalel, the divinely inspired craftsman appointed to build the Tabernacle, and we receive a renewed command to observe the Sabbath. But then the portion plunges into crisis: while Moses is still on Mount Sinai receiving the Torah, the people grow anxious, and the catastrophe of the Golden Calf unfolds. Moses descends, shatters the tablets, and then begins the long, painful process of seeking God's forgiveness on behalf of the people. Rashi, throughout this portion, is especially focused on resolving chronological puzzles, explaining unusual word choices, and probing the motivations of key figures like Aaron. He wants us to understand not just what happened, but why and how the Torah tells us about it.

<break time="1.5s" />

Let's begin with the very first verse. God tells Moses to take the sum of the children of Israel. Rashi immediately zeroes in on the phrase ki tisa, which literally means "when you lift up." But Rashi explains it doesn't mean lifting in a physical sense. It means obtaining a count, as the Aramaic translation confirms. And then comes the crucial detail: God says not to count the people directly, head by head, but to have each person give a half-shekel coin, and then count the coins. Why? Because, Rashi explains, things that have been counted are subject to the evil eye, and a direct census can bring plague. He points to the story of King David in the book of Samuel, where a direct census led to a devastating pestilence. Right from the opening verse, Rashi is teaching us that even the method of counting reveals something profound about how God protects His people.

<break time="0.75s" />

Next, let's look at one of Rashi's most vivid images in this portion. The verse says, "This shall they give," referring to the half-shekel coin. But the word "this" is strangely emphatic. Rashi explains that God actually showed Moses a kind of fiery coin, a coin made of flame, weighing half a shekel, and said, "Like this shall they give." Moses apparently had difficulty understanding exactly what was required, so God gave him a visual, supernatural demonstration. It's a small comment, but it captures something beautiful: even Moses, the greatest prophet, sometimes needed God to make the abstract concrete. And the image of a fiery coin suggests that giving is not merely a physical transaction but a spiritual act of illumination.

<break time="0.75s" />

Now we come to one of the most complex and fascinating comments in the entire portion. Rashi addresses the phrase "to make an expiation for your souls," which appears in connection with the half-shekel. He explains that the word terumah, meaning heave offering or contribution, appears three times in these verses, and each mention alludes to a different collection of funds. The first collection provided the silver for the heavy sockets that held up the walls of the Tabernacle. The second funded the communal sacrifices offered each year. And the third was the voluntary contribution for building the Tabernacle itself. In the first two, rich and poor gave equally, each a half-shekel. In the third, people gave whatever their hearts moved them to give. But here's where Rashi gets really intricate. He notes that the census connected to the sockets and the census in the book of Numbers both yielded the exact same number: six hundred and three thousand, five hundred and fifty. How is that possible if they took place in different years, and surely some nineteen-year-olds had turned twenty in the interim? Rashi's answer is elegant: the two censuses occurred in different calendar years for purposes of dating from the Exodus, which counts from the month of Nisan, but in the same year for purposes of people's ages, which are reckoned from Tishrei, the start of the creation calendar. So no one aged into eligibility between the two counts. It's classic Rashi, turning a numerical puzzle into a lesson about how multiple Jewish calendar systems work simultaneously.

<break time="0.75s" />

Let's turn now to the dramatic heart of the portion: the Golden Calf. The Torah says the people saw that Moses was delayed, using the Hebrew word boshesh. Rashi unpacks this word brilliantly. He explains that Moses had told the people he would return after forty days, but they miscounted. They included the day he ascended the mountain as day one, when in fact Moses meant forty complete days, each consisting of a night followed by a day. Since he ascended on the morning of the seventh of Sivan, that day couldn't count as a complete day because its preceding night wasn't part of the journey. So the fortieth day actually fell on the seventeenth of Tammuz, not the sixteenth as they had calculated. On the sixteenth, when Moses didn't appear by noon as expected, Satan seized the moment, throwing the world into darkness and confusion, even showing the people a vision of Moses being carried dead on a bier through the sky. This comment transforms what might seem like simple impatience into a tragic and almost forgivable miscalculation, compounded by supernatural deception.

<break time="0.75s" />

Finally, let's look at Aaron's role, which Rashi treats with remarkable nuance. When the people demand that Aaron make them gods, he tells them to bring the golden earrings from their wives and children. Rashi explains Aaron's strategy: he assumed the women and children would be reluctant to part with their jewelry, and the delay would buy enough time for Moses to return. But the men didn't wait. They tore the earrings from their own ears. Then, when Aaron saw the calf come to life, Rashi says he realized Satan's work had succeeded and he had no argument left to completely dissuade them. So he built an altar and proclaimed, "Tomorrow is a festival to the Lord," emphasizing "to the Lord," not to the calf, and "tomorrow," not today, still hoping Moses would arrive. The Midrash that Rashi cites adds another layer: Aaron had seen his nephew Hur killed by the mob for trying to stop them. He reasoned it was better for the blame to fall on him than for the people to commit the far graver sin of murdering a priest and prophet. Rashi refuses to let us see Aaron as either a simple hero or a simple villain. He is a man trapped in an impossible situation, making desperate calculations to minimize catastrophe.

<break time="1.5s" />

Stepping back, we can see several themes running through Rashi's commentary on Ki Tisa. First, there is a deep concern with God's care for Israel, even in moments of crisis. The census is taken with coins to protect the people from the evil eye; after the plague of the Golden Calf, God orders a new count, like a shepherd counting his beloved flock after a disaster. Second, Rashi is fascinated by chronology and precision. He resolves apparent contradictions between dates and numbers with extraordinary care, showing us that every detail in the Torah is deliberate and consistent. Third, Rashi shows us the complexity of human motivation. Aaron is not condemned or excused but understood. The people's sin begins not with malice but with a miscalculation of days and the terror of abandonment. For Rashi, close reading is an act of compassion: the more carefully we look at the text, the more fully we understand the people within it.
