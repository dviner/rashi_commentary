Parashah (Rashi): Pesach Shabbat Chol haMoed (Exodus 33:12-34:26)
Date: 2026-03-29

Welcome to Rashi in Brief, where each week we explore the Torah portion through the eyes of the great medieval commentator Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, known as Rashi. This week we're looking at a special reading for Pesach Shabbat Chol haMoed, drawn from Exodus chapters thirty-three and thirty-four, and Rashi has so much to show us about what's happening beneath the surface of these verses.

<break time="1.5s" />

So let me set the scene for you. This reading takes place in the aftermath of one of the most dramatic crises in the Torah: the sin of the golden calf. God is furious with the people of Israel, and Moses has been interceding on their behalf, pleading for God's continued presence among them. In our portion, Moses presses further. He asks God to reveal His ways, to show him His glory, and to recommit to walking personally among the people rather than sending an angel. God agrees, and in a stunning moment, passes before Moses while proclaiming the Thirteen Attributes of Mercy, a formula that becomes central to Jewish prayer to this day. Then in chapter thirty-four, God instructs Moses to carve new tablets to replace the ones he shattered, and reiterates key laws of the covenant. What Rashi focuses on throughout is the precise logic of Moses's arguments, the theological weight of every divine promise, and the legal precision embedded in the covenant's details.

<break time="1.5s" />

Let's walk through some of Rashi's most illuminating comments. We'll start with Moses's opening plea in Exodus thirty-three, verse twelve. Moses says to God, "See, You have said to me, 'Bring up this people,' but You have not let me know whom You will send with me." Now, God had previously told Moses He would send an angel to lead them. So hasn't God already answered the question? Rashi catches this and explains that Moses is saying: telling me You'll send an angel is not "letting me know" in any satisfying sense. Moses is unsatisfied with the angel arrangement. He wants God Himself. Right from the opening words, Rashi shows us that Moses is a tenacious negotiator, carefully parsing God's own language to press for something greater.

<break time="0.75s" />

A few verses later, in thirty-three verse sixteen, Moses asks, "For wherein shall it be known that I and Your people have found favor in Your eyes? Is it not in Your going with us?" And then Moses adds a remarkable further request: that God should not let His divine presence, the Shechinah, rest upon the other nations of the world. Rashi highlights this as a distinct, additional petition. Moses wants the relationship between God and Israel to be exclusive and distinguishing. Rashi connects the Hebrew word here, "v'niflinu," meaning "we shall be distinguished," to the same root used back in Exodus chapter nine when God separated the cattle of Israel from those of Egypt during the plagues. It's the same concept: divine distinction, divine separation. And Rashi tells us God agreed. He even brings the example of the pagan prophet Balaam, who received prophecy not through the resting of the Shechinah but in a diminished way, fallen in a kind of trance with his eyes uncovered. The exclusivity Moses asked for was granted.

<break time="0.75s" />

Now we come to one of the most theologically rich moments in all of Torah. In chapter thirty-four, verse six, God passes before Moses and proclaims: "The Lord, the Lord, God, merciful and gracious, slow to anger and abundant in kindness and truth." Rashi zeroes in on the repetition of God's name: why is "the Lord" said twice? His answer is profound. The first mention refers to God's mercy toward a person before they sin, and the second to God's mercy after they have sinned and repented. Think about what that means. God's compassion isn't only for the righteous. It's built into the very structure of divine identity, extending to the person who has fallen and is trying to get back up. Rashi also notes that the word "El," often translated as God, is here an attribute of mercy, not strict justice. His proof? The Psalmist cries out, "My God, my God, why have You forsaken me?" You wouldn't say that to a judge. You say it to someone whose nature is compassion.

<break time="0.75s" />

Staying with this proclamation, Rashi makes a stunning observation about why God teaches Moses this prayer formula in the first place. When Moses had previously interceded for Israel, he invoked the merits of the patriarchs, Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. But what happens when those merits are exhausted? God essentially says: I will teach you a new way to pray. I will pass before you and proclaim the Thirteen Attributes of Mercy, and this formula, when Israel invokes it sincerely, will never return empty. Rashi, drawing on the Talmud in tractate Rosh Hashanah, tells us that God made an irrevocable covenant: these Thirteen Attributes will always be effective. This is extraordinary. It means that the doorway of repentance and mercy is never permanently closed. No matter how dire the situation, the prayer God Himself taught Moses remains available.

<break time="0.75s" />

Moving to chapter thirty-four, verse one, God tells Moses, "Hew for yourself two tablets of stone." Rashi offers a beautiful parable to explain why Moses, not God, must carve the new tablets. He compares it to a king who went overseas, leaving his betrothed with her handmaids. The handmaids behaved immorally and the bride got a bad reputation. The bride's advocate, her bridesman, tore up the marriage contract to protect her. When the king investigated and found that only the handmaids were at fault, he reconciled with his bride. But he told the bridesman: you tore up the contract, so you buy the new paper, and I'll write on it in my own handwriting. The king is God, the bride is Israel, the handmaids are the mixed multitude who instigated the golden calf, and the bridesman is Moses, who shattered the first tablets. So God says: you broke them, you carve the new ones, and I will inscribe them. Rashi also notes that the second tablets were given quietly, privately, with no fanfare, because the great public spectacle surrounding the first tablets invited the evil eye. There is, Rashi says, no finer quality than modesty.

<break time="0.75s" />

Finally, let me highlight Rashi's comment on the thrice-repeated prohibition, "You shall not cook a kid in its mother's milk." Rashi explains that this law appears three times in the Torah, and each repetition serves a distinct legal purpose: one prohibits eating meat cooked with milk, one prohibits deriving any benefit from such a mixture, and one prohibits the act of cooking them together. He also clarifies that the word "gedi," kid, doesn't mean only a baby goat. It refers to any tender young animal, including calves and lambs. And the phrase "in its mother's milk" tells us something important: a fowl, which has no mother's milk, is not included in the biblical prohibition. The restriction on poultry and milk is a later rabbinic enactment. Rashi is meticulous about distinguishing biblical law from rabbinic law, even in a passage that seems straightforward.

<break time="1.5s" />

Stepping back, what ties all of Rashi's comments together in this portion? I think it's the theme of relationship and renewal. Moses negotiates with God not as a distant subject before a king, but as someone who knows the language of the relationship and uses it with precision and love. God responds not with rigid justice but with a formula for eternal mercy. The broken tablets are replaced, the covenant is restored, and the terms are re-established, this time more quietly, more intimately. Rashi shows us a God who wants to be asked, who rewards persistence, and who builds mechanisms of forgiveness into the very fabric of the covenant. For Rashi, every repeated word, every unusual phrase, every seeming redundancy is a doorway into these deeper truths. His method teaches us that the Torah rewards the closest possible attention.
