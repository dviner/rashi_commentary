Parashah (Rashi): Vayakhel-Pekudei (Exodus 35:1-40:38)
Date: 2026-03-13

Welcome back to Rashi in Brief, where each week we explore the Torah portion through the eyes of the greatest Jewish Bible commentator, Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, known as Rashi. This week we have a double portion called Vayakhel-Pekudei, which brings us to the grand finale of the book of Exodus, and Rashi has some truly wonderful insights to share with us.

<break time="1.5s" />

So what happens in Vayakhel-Pekudei? After the devastating incident of the golden calf and Moses's successful plea for God's forgiveness, Moses gathers the entire Israelite community and tells them to build the Mishkan, the portable sanctuary, the place where God's presence will dwell among them. The people respond with overwhelming generosity, bringing gold, silver, copper, fabrics, oils, and precious stones — so much, in fact, that Moses has to tell them to stop. Bezalel and Oholiab lead a team of skilled artisans in constructing every element: the Ark, the menorah, the altars, the priestly garments, and the structure itself. Then in Pekudei, we get an accounting of all the materials used, the final assembly of the Tabernacle, and that breathtaking moment when God's cloud descends upon it. Rashi, throughout this portion, is especially attentive to subtle word choices, grammatical oddities, and places where the Torah seems to repeat itself — but where each repetition actually carries new meaning.

<break time="1.5s" />

Let's dive into some of Rashi's most striking comments. First, right at the opening of the portion, the Torah tells us that Moses assembled the people and immediately instructed them about Shabbat, the Sabbath, before telling them about building the Tabernacle. Rashi asks: why mention Shabbat first? His answer is beautifully principled. Moses placed the Sabbath command before the building project to make clear that even this sacred work — building a home for God — does not override Shabbat. You might think that if anything could justify working on Shabbat, it would be constructing God's dwelling place. But no. Rashi tells us that the order of the commands teaches us that Shabbat takes priority. This sets a profound spiritual boundary: holiness in time comes before holiness in space.
<break time="0.75s" />
Next, let's look at one of the most emotionally powerful comments in the entire Torah. The Torah tells us that the copper laver, the washing basin used by the priests, was made from the mirrors of the women who crowded at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting. Rashi tells us that Moses initially wanted to reject these mirrors because they seemed to be instruments of vanity. But God told Moses: accept them, these are dearer to me than all the other contributions. Why? Because back in Egypt, when the Israelite men were broken and exhausted from their slave labor, the women would bring them food and drink, then take out their mirrors and playfully say to their husbands, "Look, I am more beautiful than you!" Through this tenderness and affection, they rekindled their husbands' desire to live, to love, and to bring children into the world. Those mirrors, Rashi tells us, were the instruments through which the great multitudes of Israel were raised up. What seems like vanity was actually an act of deep love and national survival. And fittingly, the laver made from those mirrors would later be used in a ritual to restore peace between a husband and wife who had been estranged by jealousy.
<break time="0.75s" />
Now let's talk about the princes, the tribal leaders. The Torah spells their title, nesi'im, in a defective way, missing a letter. Rashi picks up on this and tells us a striking story. When the call went out for contributions, the princes said: let the rest of the people give first, and whatever is lacking, we will supply. It sounds reasonable, even generous. But the people gave so wholeheartedly that everything was provided, and the princes were left with almost nothing to contribute — just the precious stones and the oil. Because they were slow to act, because they held back when enthusiasm was called for, the Torah itself diminishes their name by dropping a letter. The lesson is sharp: in matters of holiness, don't calculate — step forward.
<break time="0.75s" />
Rashi also draws our attention to Bezalel and his partner Oholiab. Bezalel came from the tribe of Judah, one of the most prominent tribes. Oholiab came from the tribe of Dan, one of the lowest in status, descended from one of the handmaids. Yet God placed them as equals in the sacred work. Rashi explains that this was deliberate, to teach that God does not regard the rich more than the poor. The construction of God's dwelling place required partnership across social boundaries. Sacred work demands dignity for all who participate.
<break time="0.75s" />
There's a wonderful comment about Bezalel's intuition. The Torah says that Bezalel made all that God commanded Moses — not all that Moses commanded Bezalel, but all that God commanded Moses. Rashi explains the significance: even things that Moses hadn't explicitly told him, Bezalel arrived at independently. When Moses told him to make the vessels first and the Tabernacle structure second, Bezalel respectfully pushed back, saying: isn't it the way of the world to build the house first and then bring in the furniture? Moses was amazed and said: you must have been standing in the shadow of God — a play on Bezalel's name, which means "in the shadow of God" — because that is exactly what God told me. This comment reveals something beautiful about the nature of wisdom: true understanding isn't just following instructions, it's grasping the inner logic of what you've been asked to do.
<break time="0.75s" />
Finally, at the very end of the portion, we encounter an apparent contradiction. One verse says Moses could not enter the Tent of Meeting because the cloud of God's presence rested upon it. But elsewhere in the Torah, we read that Moses did enter the Tent to speak with God. How can both be true? Rashi resolves this elegantly: when the cloud was upon the tent, Moses could not enter. When the cloud withdrew, he entered and God spoke with him. It's a simple answer, but it preserves the dignity of both verses and reveals the dynamic, living quality of the relationship between God and Moses — a relationship of invitation and withdrawal, presence and mystery.

<break time="1.5s" />

Stepping back, what themes emerge from Rashi's commentary across this double portion? One theme is the profound value of wholehearted participation. Whether it's the women with their mirrors, the people who gave so generously that Moses had to stop them, or Bezalel who threw himself into the work more completely than anyone else — Rashi consistently highlights the spiritual power of giving fully and immediately. A second theme is the democratization of holiness: the mirrors of ordinary women become sacred vessels, a craftsman from the lowliest tribe stands equal to one from the noblest, and the entire community participates in building God's home. And a third theme, characteristic of Rashi throughout the Torah, is his insistence that every word matters. A missing letter in a title, a subtle shift in verb form, the order of two commands — none of it is accidental. For Rashi, the Torah is a text where meaning lives not just in grand narratives, but in the smallest details.
