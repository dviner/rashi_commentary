Parashah (Rashi): Shmini (Leviticus 9:1-11:47)
Date: 2026-04-05

Welcome back to Rashi in Brief, where each week we explore the Torah portion through the eyes of the master commentator, Rashi. This week's portion is called Shmini, meaning "eighth," and it covers some of the most dramatic and emotionally intense moments in the entire book of Leviticus. Let's see what Rashi helps us discover.

<break time="1.5s" />

Parashat Shmini, found in Leviticus chapters nine through eleven, begins on the eighth day following the seven-day inauguration of the Tabernacle, the portable sanctuary the Israelites built in the wilderness. It's the day everything has been building toward: Aaron the High Priest will perform his first sacrificial service, and God's presence, the Shechinah, will finally descend to rest among the people. But the joy of this momentous day is shattered when two of Aaron's sons, Nadav and Avihu, bring an unauthorized fire offering and are consumed by a heavenly fire. The portion then shifts dramatically, moving from this tragedy into the laws of kashrut, the dietary laws that define which animals, fish, and birds the Israelites may and may not eat. Rashi's commentary across this portion is remarkably rich, addressing questions of emotional nuance in the narrative sections, legal precision in the ritual details, and theological depth in the dietary laws. He is especially attuned to the inner lives of Moses and Aaron, reading between the lines to find the anxiety, grief, and humility that the spare biblical text only hints at.

<break time="1.5s" />

Let's start at the very beginning of the portion. The Torah says Moses tells Aaron to take a calf for a sin offering. Why specifically a calf? Rashi explains that the calf was chosen deliberately to signal to Aaron that God was granting him atonement for the sin of the golden calf, the idol Aaron had helped fashion in the wilderness. This is a stunning observation. The very animal that symbolized Aaron's greatest failure is now the instrument of his forgiveness. Rashi is showing us that the Torah's word choices are never accidental: every detail in the sacrificial service carries emotional and spiritual weight.

<break time="0.75s" />

Next, when Moses instructs Aaron to approach the altar, Rashi notices something surprising. He says Aaron was diffident, meaning shy or reluctant, and afraid to step forward. Moses had to encourage him, saying, in effect, "Why are you holding back? This is exactly what you were chosen for!" Rashi is drawing from the Midrash here, but the insight is psychologically profound. Aaron, on what should be the proudest day of his life, is paralyzed by self-doubt, perhaps still haunted by the golden calf incident. Rashi reminds us that spiritual leadership doesn't come with automatic confidence; even the High Priest needed a push.

<break time="0.75s" />

Now we come to the tragedy. After Nadav and Avihu die, the Torah records one of the most powerful silences in all of Scripture: "Vayidom Aharon," and Aaron was silent. Rashi comments that Aaron received a reward for this silence. What was the reward? God subsequently addressed Aaron directly and privately, speaking the laws about priests not drinking wine before serving in the Tabernacle to Aaron alone, without going through Moses as an intermediary. This is extraordinary. In almost every other case in the Torah, God speaks to Moses, who then relays the message. But here, Aaron's dignified acceptance of his devastating loss earns him a unique, direct communication from God. Rashi is teaching us that sometimes the most eloquent response to suffering is silence, and that such restraint is not passivity but a form of spiritual greatness.

<break time="0.75s" />

Shortly after the tragedy, a legal dispute arises between Moses and Aaron that Rashi unpacks in beautiful detail. Moses discovers that the goat of the sin offering for the New Moon has been burned rather than eaten by the priests, and he is upset. Rashi explains that Moses directed his anger at Aaron's surviving sons, Elazar and Itamar, out of respect for Aaron, even though Aaron himself may have been responsible. When Aaron finally speaks up, he explains his reasoning: the command Moses gave to eat offerings despite being in mourning applied only to the special, one-time offerings of the inauguration day, not to the regular New Moon offering, which would be obligatory for all future generations. You had no right, Aaron essentially argues, to extend a lenient ruling from a temporary situation to a permanent one. And Rashi tells us that when Moses heard this, "it was pleasing in his eyes." Moses admitted his error and was not ashamed to say so. Rather than claiming he had never received a teaching on this matter, Moses openly acknowledged that he had heard the ruling but forgotten it. Rashi holds this up as a model of intellectual honesty.

<break time="0.75s" />

Finally, when the Torah transitions to the dietary laws, Rashi makes a remarkable comment on the very first verse. The Torah uses the word "chayah," meaning living creature, rather than the more common "behemah," meaning animal. Rashi explains this word choice with a beautiful parable. Because Israel cleaves to God, they deserve life, and so God gave them commandments about what to eat and what not to eat. It's like a physician, Rashi says, who visits two patients. To the one who has no hope of recovery, the doctor says eat whatever you like. But to the one who will live, the doctor gives careful instructions about diet. The dietary laws, in Rashi's reading, are not restrictions but signs of care, evidence that God sees Israel as worth investing in. Rashi also notes that Moses physically held up each animal and showed it to the people, saying "this you may eat" and "this you may not eat," making the teaching as concrete and visual as possible.

<break time="0.75s" />

One more comment worth noting concerns the prohibition against touching animal carcasses. The Torah says "you shall not touch their carcasses." Rashi asks the obvious question: does this really mean Israelites can never touch a dead animal? He answers no. By comparing this to the stricter laws about priests and human corpses, Rashi demonstrates through a fortiori reasoning that if even the more severe impurity of a human corpse is only restricted for priests, then surely the lesser impurity of an animal carcass cannot be universally prohibited. The verse, Rashi concludes, refers specifically to the festivals, when every Israelite was required to appear at the Sanctuary in a state of ritual purity. This is classic Rashi: taking what seems like an absolute rule and showing, through careful legal reasoning, its precise and limited scope.

<break time="1.5s" />

Stepping back, several themes emerge from Rashi's commentary on this portion. One is the theme of inner struggle behind sacred duty: Aaron's reluctance at the altar, his silence after tragedy, his careful legal reasoning even in the depths of grief. Rashi consistently reads the Torah as a document about real human beings wrestling with enormous spiritual responsibilities. Another theme is intellectual humility. Moses admits he was wrong. Aaron earns divine favor through silence rather than protest. Rashi values the courage it takes to accept correction and to restrain oneself. And in the dietary laws, Rashi reveals a third theme: that commandments are expressions of divine love and care, not arbitrary burdens. Throughout, Rashi models a way of reading that is simultaneously precise and compassionate, attending to every word of the text while never losing sight of the people behind the words.
