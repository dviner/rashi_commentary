Parashah (Rashi): Bamidbar (Numbers 1:1-4:20)
Date: 2026-05-10

Welcome to Rashi in Brief, where each week we explore the Torah portion through the eyes of the great medieval commentator, Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, known simply as Rashi. This week's portion is Bamidbar, the opening of the Book of Numbers, and Rashi has so much to show us about what's really happening beneath the surface of this text.

<break time="1.5s" />

Bamidbar means "in the wilderness," and that's exactly where the Israelites find themselves — camped in the desert of Sinai, about a year after leaving Egypt. God commands Moses to take a census of the entire people, tribe by tribe, counting every male from twenty years old and upward. Then God lays out a detailed plan for how the tribes should arrange themselves around the Tabernacle — the portable sanctuary — in a specific formation of four camps, each with its own banner. The Levites are singled out for special duty: they won't be counted with the rest of the people but will be assigned to care for and transport the Tabernacle. So there's a lot of counting, organizing, and assigning of roles. What Rashi zeroes in on throughout this portion are the questions you might not think to ask: Why count the people again? Why are the Levites treated differently? What does each small word or phrase add that we might otherwise overlook? His commentary transforms what could feel like a dry census into a deeply meaningful portrait of God's relationship with Israel.

<break time="1.5s" />

Let's start at the very beginning. The Torah opens by telling us that God spoke to Moses in the wilderness of Sinai, on the first day of the second month, commanding him to count the people. The obvious question is: why another census? God already counted the Israelites when they left Egypt, and again after the sin of the golden calf. Rashi's answer is beautifully simple — because they were dear to Him, He counts them constantly. Just as a person who treasures something counts it again and again, God counts Israel at every pivotal moment: when they left Egypt, after the golden calf to see how many remained, and now, as He is about to cause His divine presence to dwell among them in the newly erected Tabernacle. The census isn't bureaucratic. It's an act of love.

<break time="0.75s" />

Next, let's look at how the people proved their tribal lineage. The Torah says the Israelites "declared their pedigrees after their families." That sounds straightforward enough, but Rashi explains that they actually brought documents — records of their genealogy — along with witnesses to confirm their parentage. Each person had to formally establish which tribe they belonged to. And Rashi adds an important legal principle: a person whose father belongs to one tribe and whose mother to another follows the father's tribe. This matters because the entire structure of the camp and the census depends on clear tribal identity. What might seem like a simple phrase actually reflects a rigorous, organized process of national self-definition.

<break time="0.75s" />

Now here's one of the most important comments in this portion — why the Levites are excluded from the general census. The Torah says plainly, "The tribe of Levi thou shalt not number among them." Rashi offers two explanations. First, a poetic one: the legion of the King deserves to be counted separately. The Levites serve God directly, so they get their own special census. But then Rashi offers a second, more dramatic explanation. God foresaw that everyone counted in this census — those twenty years and older — would eventually be condemned to die in the wilderness after the sin of the spies. So God said, "Let the Levites not be included, because they are Mine. They did not sin with the golden calf, and they should not fall under this future decree." The exclusion from the census is actually an act of divine protection. What looks like a technicality turns out to be an act of mercy rooted in God's foreknowledge.

<break time="0.75s" />

One of my favorite Rashi comments in this portion concerns the arrangement of the camps. The Torah says the Israelites shall camp "every man by his own standard, with the signs of their fathers' houses." What were these signs? Rashi explains that each tribal banner had a distinctive colored cloth, and the color of each tribe's banner matched the color of that tribe's stone in the High Priest's breastplate. So there was a visual system of identification — you could look across the camp and know exactly which tribe was where by the color of its flag. But Rashi adds a second explanation that reaches back to the Book of Genesis. These positions, he says, followed the instructions that Jacob himself gave his sons when he told them how to carry his coffin out of Egypt. Jacob assigned Judah, Issachar, and Zebulun to the east, Reuben, Simeon, and Gad to the south, and so on. The camp arrangement in the wilderness, then, is not arbitrary — it echoes the dying wishes of their ancestor. The wilderness camp is a living memory of Jacob's final blessing.

<break time="0.75s" />

Here's a comment that carries a powerful moral lesson. Rashi notes that the family of Kohath, from the tribe of Levi, was assigned to camp on the south side of the Tabernacle. And who else was camped on the south side? The tribe of Reuben. Rashi then quotes a striking saying: "Woe to the wicked, woe to his neighbor." Because members of the tribe of Reuben — specifically Dathan, Abiram, and two hundred fifty men — were camped near Korah and his family among the Kohathites, they were eventually drawn into Korah's rebellion against Moses and perished along with him. Proximity matters. Your neighbors influence you. But Rashi immediately offers the flip side. Moses, Aaron, and his sons camped on the east side, and next to them was the tribe of Judah, along with Issachar and Zebulun. And the saying here is: "Well is it with the righteous, well with his neighbor." Because they were neighbors of Moses, who occupied himself with Torah, these tribes became great scholars of the Law. Rashi turns the camp layout into a meditation on the power of environment and influence.

<break time="0.75s" />

Finally, let's look at one of the most touching details in the portion. When the Torah lists the offspring of Aaron and Moses, it only actually names Aaron's sons — Nadab, Abihu, Eleazar, and Ithamar. So why does the Torah call them the offspring of Moses too? Rashi's answer is profound: because Moses taught them Torah. And whoever teaches Torah to the child of his fellow, Scripture regards it as though he had begotten that child. This redefines parenthood. Biological connection is one kind of bond, but the transmission of wisdom and values creates a bond equally real, equally sacred. Rashi finds this extraordinary principle tucked inside what looks like a simple genealogical heading.

<break time="1.5s" />

Stepping back from these individual comments, several themes emerge in Rashi's reading of Bamidbar. The first is that counting is never just counting — it always expresses relationship, whether God's love for Israel or the careful ordering of a community. The second is that physical arrangement matters deeply. Where you camp, who your neighbors are, what colors mark your banner — all of these carry spiritual weight and moral consequence. The third is a theme of protection and foresight. God protects the Levites by excluding them from a census that will later carry a death sentence; God structures the camp to safeguard the sacred. And running through everything is Rashi's characteristic method: finding the question hidden inside what seems obvious, and showing us that every word of Torah is there for a reason.
