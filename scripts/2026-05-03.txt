Parashah (Rashi): Behar-Bechukotai (Leviticus 25:1-27:34)
Date: 2026-05-03

Welcome to Rashi in Brief, where each week we explore the Torah portion through the eyes of the great medieval commentator, Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, known as Rashi. This week we're looking at the double portion of Behar-Bechukotai, from the book of Leviticus, and as always, Rashi has a remarkable ability to show us what's hiding just beneath the surface of the text.

<break time="1.5s" />

Behar-Bechukotai covers some of the Torah's most distinctive economic and social legislation. Behar introduces the Shemittah, the sabbatical year when the land of Israel must rest from agricultural work, and the Yovel, the jubilee year that occurs every fiftieth year, when sold land returns to its original owners and Hebrew servants go free. It also addresses laws about fair commerce, the prohibition against charging interest, and the treatment of those who fall into poverty. Bechukotai then lays out the blessings that follow from observing these laws and the severe consequences of neglecting them. Rashi, working through this material, zeroes in on precisely the kinds of puzzles he loves: a seemingly unnecessary phrase, a word that could be read two ways, a logical gap that needs filling. He asks why certain details are repeated, what specific words really mean, and how the Torah's laws connect to one another in ways we might not immediately see.

<break time="1.5s" />

Let's begin with the very first words of the portion. The Torah says, "And the Lord spoke to Moses on Mount Sinai," and then proceeds to give the laws of the sabbatical year. Rashi asks what has become one of the most famous questions in all of Torah commentary: What does the sabbatical year have to do with Mount Sinai? After all, weren't all the commandments given at Sinai? Why single this one out? Rashi explains that the Torah mentions Sinai here specifically because the laws of Shemittah are presented with their general principles and their finest details all together. This teaches us that just as the sabbatical year was given in full at Sinai, so too were all the commandments, down to their most minute specifications, given at Sinai. It's a sweeping statement about the completeness of revelation, derived from just two seemingly extra words.

<break time="0.75s" />

Next, let's look at how Rashi handles the sabbatical year's produce. The Torah says you shall not reap the aftergrowth of your harvest and you shall not gather the grapes of your untended vines. Rashi is careful to point out that this doesn't mean you can't eat the produce at all. Rather, you shall not reap it as your exclusive property the way you would in an ordinary year. The produce must be declared ownerless, free for anyone to take. Then, Rashi says, you too may eat from it. He draws this from a subtle reading of the phrase "the sabbath of the land shall be food for you." Notice the Torah doesn't say "the produce of the land" but "the sabbath of the land," meaning only produce that has been treated according to the sabbatical law, that has been made available to all, may you eat. If you hoard it, you've violated the very spirit of the year.

<break time="0.75s" />

One of Rashi's most practical and poignant comments comes on the laws of buying and selling land. The Torah warns, "You shall not wrong one another," and Rashi distinguishes between two kinds of wrongdoing. In one verse, the phrase refers to financial fraud, specifically overcharging for land by ignoring how many productive years remain until the jubilee. But when the Torah repeats the phrase a few verses later, "You shall not wrong one another," Rashi says this second instance refers to something different entirely: verbal wrongs, hurting someone's feelings, or giving advice that serves your own interests rather than theirs. And because no one can truly know another person's intentions, the Torah adds the warning, "But you shall fear your God." Rashi explains that whenever a matter is entrusted to the heart alone, where only God can see the truth, the Torah invokes this phrase. It's a profound insight into the Torah's moral psychology, the recognition that some of the worst wrongs are invisible to everyone except the one committing them and God.

<break time="0.75s" />

Rashi also gives us a beautiful image when discussing the obligation to help someone who is falling into poverty. The Torah says, "If your brother grows poor, you shall relieve him." Rashi reads this as a command to act immediately, at the very first sign of financial difficulty, before the person has fully collapsed. He offers a parable: Think of an overloaded donkey. While the load is still on the donkey's back, one person can grab hold and steady it. But once the donkey has fallen to the ground, even five people cannot lift it back up. The message is unmistakable: don't wait. Intervene early, when your help can still make a difference. It's a comment that transforms a legal obligation into a vivid moral imperative.

<break time="0.75s" />

Finally, let's look at how Rashi treats the laws of the Hebrew servant who is sold to a non-Jewish master. The Torah insists that such a servant must be redeemed, and Rashi says emphatically, "He shall be redeemed at once. Do not allow him to totally sink his identity among the heathens." But Rashi also insists on fairness in the other direction. When calculating the redemption price, the master must receive honest payment for the remaining years of service. Rashi works through the arithmetic in detail: if the servant was purchased for twenty manehs to work twenty years, and has served five, then fifteen manehs must be repaid. You must not cheat the non-Jewish master, Rashi explains, because doing so would constitute a desecration of God's name. This is striking. Even in a case where the Torah is clearly prioritizing the freedom of the Jewish servant, Rashi insists that justice must be done to all parties, because God's reputation in the world depends on how His people conduct themselves.

<break time="1.5s" />

Stepping back, several themes emerge from Rashi's commentary on this portion. First, there is the idea of completeness and divine authority. That opening question about Sinai establishes that every detail of Torah law carries the weight of direct revelation. Second, there is a deep concern with economic justice, not just in the technical rules of buying and selling, but in the moral sensibility behind them. Rashi shows us a Torah that cares about feelings as much as finances, about intention as much as action. Third, there is the recurring insistence that sanctity and practical life are inseparable. The sabbatical year is not just an agricultural regulation; it reshapes how people relate to property, to each other, and to God. And finally, Rashi reveals his conviction that the Torah's laws are meant to create a society of mutual responsibility, one where you catch someone before they fall, where freedom is protected, and where even your dealings with outsiders must reflect integrity and the honor of heaven.
