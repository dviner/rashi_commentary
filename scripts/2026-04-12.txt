Parashah (Rashi): Tazria-Metzora (Leviticus 12:1-15:33)
Date: 2026-04-12

Welcome to Rashi in Brief, the podcast where each week we explore the Torah portion through the eyes of the great medieval commentator Rashi, Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki. This week we're looking at the double portion of Tazria-Metzora, from the book of Leviticus, a section that might seem technical at first glance, but through Rashi's eyes reveals profound lessons about human nature, community, and the power of speech.

<break time="1.5s" />

Tazria-Metzora covers some of the most detailed ritual legislation in the entire Torah. The portion begins with laws of ritual impurity following childbirth, then moves into the extensive and intricate laws of tzaraat — often translated as leprosy, though it's really a spiritual condition that manifests as discolorations on the skin, on garments, and even on the walls of houses. The Torah describes how a person suspected of tzaraat must be examined by a kohen, a priest, who determines whether the affliction is ritually impure. If declared impure, the metzora — the afflicted person — must live in isolation outside the camp. The portion then describes the elaborate purification process involving birds, cedar wood, crimson wool, and hyssop. Rashi, throughout this portion, is deeply interested in the precise mechanics of these laws — why certain words appear, what each detail teaches us legally, and what spiritual meaning lies beneath what can feel like a very clinical surface.

<break time="1.5s" />

Let's begin with the very first verse. The Torah says, "If a woman conceives and gives birth to a male child." Rashi, citing Rabbi Simlai, asks a structural question: why does the Torah place the laws about human impurity here, right after the laws about kosher and non-kosher animals in the previous chapter? His answer is elegant. Just as in the creation story, human beings were formed after all the animals, beasts, and birds, so too the Torah's laws regarding humans are set forth after those regarding animals. This is Rashi pointing us to a deep symmetry between the order of creation and the order of law, reminding us that the Torah is not arranged randomly — its structure mirrors the very architecture of the world.

<break time="0.75s" />

Next, let's look at one of the most striking details in the laws of tzaraat. The Torah says that if a person's leprosy spreads to cover his entire body from head to toe, the priest shall pronounce him clean. That seems completely counterintuitive — the more the disease spreads, the cleaner the person is? Rashi doesn't shy away from this. He treats it as established law. But this principle becomes important later, because Rashi explains that the same logic applies to garments affected by tzaraat. If the discoloration spreads over the entire garment, it is clean. How does Rashi know this? Through a gezeirah shavah, a hermeneutical rule connecting similar phrases. The Torah uses the terms karachat and gabachat — terms for baldness on the back and front of the head — both in the laws of human leprosy and in the laws of garment leprosy. Just as full-body spreading on a person means cleanness, so too full-garment spreading means cleanness. It's a wonderful example of how Rashi shows the Torah's legal system working as an interconnected web.

<break time="0.75s" />

Now, one of Rashi's most famous comments in this portion concerns the metzora's isolation. The Torah says the afflicted person "shall dwell alone, outside the camp." Rashi asks a pointed question: other ritually impure people are also sent out of certain camps, so why is the metzora singled out to dwell completely alone, separated even from other impure individuals? His answer cuts to the heart of the matter. Because the metzora, through lashon hara — slanderous or malicious speech — caused separation between husbands and wives, between friends, he himself must now be separated from everyone. The punishment mirrors the crime. This is Rashi at his moral best, drawing out the Torah's ethical logic from what appears to be a dry ritual instruction. The isolation isn't arbitrary; it's a kind of spiritual therapy, forcing the person to experience the loneliness they inflicted on others.

<break time="0.75s" />

This theme of speech continues powerfully in the purification ritual. When the metzora is ready to be purified, the Torah requires him to bring two live, ritually clean birds. Why birds, of all creatures? Rashi explains: because the plagues of leprosy come as a punishment for slander, which is an act of chattering, therefore birds are required for purification, because birds chatter continuously with a twittering sound. The very instrument of the person's downfall — excessive, harmful speech — becomes the symbolic vehicle of their healing. But Rashi doesn't stop there. The Torah also requires cedar wood, crimson wool, and hyssop. Rashi explains that the cedar, a tall and lofty tree, is used because plagues also come as punishment for arrogance and haughtiness. And the remedy? The crimson wool, called sheni tolaat — literally involving a worm — and the lowly hyssop plant. The message is clear: the afflicted person must humble themselves, seeing themselves as lowly as a worm and as modest as hyssop. It's a complete arc from pride and harmful speech to humility and healing.

<break time="0.75s" />

Finally, let's look at a smaller but revealing comment. During the purification process, the Torah says the metzora shall shave all his hair and then "dwell outside his tent" for seven days. Rashi zeroes in on this phrase — "outside his tent." He explains that this means marital relations are forbidden to the metzora during this period. The tent, in Torah language, often refers to one's domestic and intimate life, as we see elsewhere when God tells the Israelites to "return to your tents." So even after the metzora has begun the purification process, even after re-entering the camp, there remains a period of separation from the most intimate human bond. The healing, Rashi implies, is not instantaneous. Restoration to full community and relationship is a gradual process that requires patience and continued discipline.

<break time="1.5s" />

Stepping back from these individual comments, several powerful themes emerge from Rashi's treatment of Tazria-Metzora. First, there is his insistence that the Torah's ritual details carry moral weight. The affliction of tzaraat is not merely a skin disease requiring medical attention — it is a spiritual condition with ethical causes and symbolic remedies. Second, Rashi consistently shows how the Torah's punishments and purification rituals mirror the underlying offenses: isolation for the one who caused isolation, chattering birds for the one who chattered harmfully, humbling symbols for the one who was haughty. Third, Rashi reveals the Torah as a tightly woven legal and literary document, where the placement of a section, the choice of a word, even the presence or absence of a single Hebrew letter carries meaning. And perhaps most importantly for us, Rashi's commentary transforms what might seem like the most inaccessible portion of the Torah into a meditation on speech, humility, community, and the long but hopeful road of repentance and restoration.
